Lisbon

Introduction & My Personal Take

Lisbon is one of those cities that effortlessly marries ancient charm with modern vibrancy. Strolling through the winding alleys of the Alfama district, I couldn’t help but feel like I was stepping back in time—until I passed a hipster café selling artisanal pastel de nata. And let me tell you, that pastel de nata was worth every bite of guilt.

I visited Lisbon in the spring of 2023, and despite having traveled to over 40 countries, this city still managed to wow me. Between the hilltop views from Castelo de São Jorge, the rhythmic clatter of Tram 28, and the mouthwatering aromas wafting from every corner, Lisbon felt like a constant invitation to explore further.

History

Lisbon’s history spans over 2,000 years, making it one of Europe’s oldest capitals. A major trading hub during the Age of Discovery, Portuguese explorers set sail from its shores, forever changing the course of world history. Earthquakes, rebuilds, and expansions have shaped the city into a patchwork of architectural styles—Gothic cathedrals, Baroque facades, and modern street art all coexist here.

Population

Lisbon’s city limits house around 550,000 people, but the metropolitan area swells to over 2.8 million. Yet, despite its size, the city’s numerous miradouros (viewpoints) and intimate plazas give it a cozy feel, as if each neighborhood is a small village.

Notable Attractions

  • Belém Tower: A UNESCO World Heritage site marking Portugal’s maritime heritage.
  • Jerónimos Monastery: A stunning example of Manueline architecture—intricate, grand, and very Portuguese.
  • São Jorge Castle: Perched atop a hill, offering panoramic views of the city’s red-tiled rooftops.
  • Alfama District: The oldest neighborhood in Lisbon, famed for Fado music and labyrinthine alleys.
  • Lisbon Oceanarium: One of Europe’s largest aquariums, perfect for families or rainy days.

Best Time to Visit

Spring (March to May) and autumn (September to October) are ideal, with warm weather and fewer crowds. Summers can be hot and busy, while winters are mild but rainy. If you can, time your visit around the Festas de Lisboa in June for a truly festive atmosphere.

Prices Overview

Lisbon is relatively budget-friendly compared to other European capitals. Hostels start around €15 per night, while mid-range hotels can be found for €60-150. Dining ranges from €10 for a hearty local meal to €30 or more for upscale restaurants. Street food—like the iconic pastel de nata—often costs just a couple of euros.

Fun Facts

  • Lisbon’s famous yellow Tram 28 runs through the city’s historic districts, tackling impossibly steep hills and tight turns.
  • The city is built on seven hills—so wear comfortable shoes or hop on a tram.
  • Lisbon was nearly destroyed by a massive earthquake in 1755, leading to a major urban redesign.
  • Fado music, a soulful genre born in Lisbon, has been recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage.
  • Belém pastries (pastéis de Belém) originated in the 19th century, and the recipe is still a guarded secret.

Quick Tips

Currency: Euro (€). Credit cards widely accepted, but carry small cash for markets.

Language: Portuguese, though English is common in tourist areas.

Time Zone: Western European Time (GMT), UTC+0.

Climate: Mediterranean—mild winters, hot summers.
