What to See

Tokyo, the vibrant capital of Japan, seamlessly blends the ultra-modern with deep-rooted traditions. From towering skyscrapers and bustling shopping districts to serene temples and picturesque gardens, Tokyo offers a diverse array of attractions. Visitors can experience the cutting-edge technology in Akihabara, the historic charm of Asakusa, the tranquil beauty of the Imperial Palace Gardens, and the lively atmosphere of Shibuya Crossing.

A Brief History of the City

Originally a small fishing village named Edo, Tokyo rose to prominence when it became the seat of the Tokugawa shogunate in the early 17th century. Edo thrived as a cultural and economic hub until the Meiji Restoration in 1868, which led to its renaming as Tokyo, meaning "Eastern Capital." Throughout the 20th century, Tokyo underwent rapid modernization and reconstruction, especially after the devastation of World War II. Today, Tokyo stands as a symbol of Japan's resilience, innovation, and rich cultural heritage.

Top 10 Attractions

  1. Shibuya Crossing - Often called the busiest pedestrian crossing in the world, it's an iconic symbol of Tokyo's bustling urban life.
  2. Tokyo Tower - Inspired by the Eiffel Tower, it offers panoramic views of the city and houses various attractions.
  3. Senso-ji Temple - Tokyo's oldest and most significant Buddhist temple, located in the historic Asakusa district.
  4. Imperial Palace and East Gardens - The primary residence of the Emperor of Japan, surrounded by beautiful gardens open to the public.
  5. Akihabara Electric Town - A paradise for electronics enthusiasts and fans of anime, manga, and gaming.
  6. Tsukiji Outer Market - Formerly home to the world-famous Tsukiji Fish Market, now a vibrant area for fresh seafood and street food.
  7. Meiji Shrine - A serene Shinto shrine nestled in a lush forested area, dedicated to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken.
  8. Odaiba - A futuristic man-made island offering shopping, entertainment, and stunning views of Tokyo Bay.
  9. Ginza District - Tokyo's premier shopping and dining area, known for its upscale boutiques and fine dining restaurants.
  10. Ueno Park - A spacious public park featuring museums, a zoo, and beautiful cherry blossoms in spring.

What to Bring

  • Comfortable walking shoes for exploring Tokyo's vast and varied attractions.
  • A lightweight jacket or sweater, as Tokyo's weather can vary, especially in the evenings.
  • Sun protection: sunscreen, hats, and sunglasses to shield against Tokyo's sunny days.
  • A reusable water bottle to stay hydrated during your adventures.
  • Camera or smartphone for capturing Tokyo's stunning skyline and memorable moments.
  • Guidebook or travel app to navigate the city's extensive public transportation system and discover hidden gems.
  • Portable charger for your electronic devices to keep them powered throughout the day.
  • Umbrella or raincoat for unexpected showers, especially during the rainy season.
  • Basic Japanese phrases guide to enhance communication with locals.
  • Cash in Japanese Yen for places that may prefer cash payments over cards.

Currency

Tokyo, like the rest of Japan, uses the Japanese Yen (¥). While credit and debit cards are widely accepted in urban areas, it's advisable to carry some cash for smaller establishments, street vendors, and certain areas where card payments may be limited.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Tokyo is during the spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November) months when the weather is mild and the city is adorned with beautiful cherry blossoms and vibrant autumn foliage. Summer (June to August) can be hot and humid, while winter (December to February) offers cooler temperatures and fewer tourists, making it ideal for exploring the city's attractions without the crowds.

Fun Facts

  • Tokyo is home to the world's busiest pedestrian crossing, Shibuya Crossing.
  • The city boasts over 230 Michelin-starred restaurants, the highest number in the world.
  • Tokyo Tower was the tallest structure in Japan until the completion of Tokyo Skytree in 2012.
  • The famous Pokémon Center is located in Tokyo, offering exclusive merchandise for fans.
  • Tokyo's subway system is one of the most efficient and punctual in the world.
  • There are over 1,500 convenience stores (konbini) scattered throughout the city.
  • Ueno Park hosts one of the largest cherry blossom festivals in Japan each spring.
  • Tokyo Disneyland and DisneySea are among the most visited theme parks globally.
  • The city has more Michelin-starred restaurants than any other city in the world.
  • Tokyo was the first city in Asia to host the Olympic Games, in 1964.

Tips and Recommendations

Do’s and Don’ts

  • Do: Utilize Tokyo's efficient public transportation system, including subways and trains, to navigate the city seamlessly.
  • Don’t: Talk loudly on public transportation. Maintain a quiet and respectful demeanor.
  • Do: Try local Japanese cuisine, including sushi, ramen, and tempura, to fully experience Tokyo's culinary delights.
  • Don’t: Litter or disturb public spaces. Tokyo prides itself on its cleanliness.
  • Do: Carry cash as some places may not accept credit cards, especially smaller eateries and shops.
  • Don’t: Miss out on exploring lesser-known neighborhoods like Shimokitazawa or Yanaka for a more authentic experience.

Packing Lists

  • Comfortable walking shoes or sneakers for extensive exploration.
  • Lightweight, breathable clothing suitable for Tokyo's varied climate.
  • Sun protection: sunscreen, hats, and sunglasses.
  • Reusable water bottle to stay hydrated throughout the day.
  • Camera or smartphone with ample storage for capturing memories.
  • Portable charger for electronic devices to keep them powered.
  • Umbrella or raincoat for unexpected showers.
  • Basic first-aid kit for minor health needs.
  • Guidebook or travel app for navigation and information.
  • Cash in Japanese Yen for places that prefer cash payments.

Local Etiquette

Japanese culture emphasizes politeness, respect, and harmony. Here are some local etiquette tips to enhance your visit to Tokyo:

  • Always greet with a polite bow and a friendly "Konnichiwa" (Hello).
  • Remove your shoes before entering someone's home or certain traditional establishments.
  • Handle money and items with both hands as a sign of respect.
  • Speak softly in public places to maintain a peaceful environment.
  • When visiting temples or shrines, follow the proper purification rituals and dress modestly.
  • Refrain from eating or drinking while walking on the streets. Consume your food in designated areas.
