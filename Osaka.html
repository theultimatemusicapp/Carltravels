What to See

Osaka is affectionately known as the "Nation’s Kitchen," celebrated for its bustling food scene, vibrant nightlife, and fascinating mix of modern architecture and historic landmarks. Stroll through Dotonbori to experience neon lights and mouthwatering street food, or visit Osaka Castle for a glimpse into Japan’s feudal past. Whether you’re exploring the cozy alleyways of Shinsekai or enjoying panoramic views atop Umeda Sky Building, this city never runs out of surprises.

A Brief History of the City

Osaka has played a significant role in Japan’s history, especially as a commercial center during the Edo period. Rebuilt after heavy wartime damage, the city has emerged as a resilient and innovative powerhouse, reflecting a spirit that continues to draw visitors from all over the world. Its past is woven into every district, from traditional temples to lively modern hubs.

Top 10 Attractions

  1. Osaka Castle – A symbol of the city’s heritage, surrounded by scenic gardens.
  2. Dotonbori – Neon lights, street food, and the iconic Glico sign await you here.
  3. Universal Studios Japan – World-class rides and themed areas for all ages.
  4. Shinsaibashi Shopping Arcade – Shop till you drop and indulge in local treats.
  5. Umeda Sky Building – Catch spectacular city views from the Floating Garden Observatory.
  6. Tsutenkaku Tower – An old-school landmark offering a nostalgic vibe in Shinsekai.
  7. Sumiyoshi Taisha – One of Japan’s oldest Shinto shrines with striking architecture.
  8. Osaka Aquarium Kaiyukan – Home to whale sharks and other marine wonders.
  9. Tempozan Ferris Wheel – Enjoy a relaxing ride overlooking Osaka Bay.
  10. National Bunraku Theatre – Discover Japan’s traditional puppet theatre in its prime.

What to Bring

  • Weather-appropriate clothing (Osaka can be humid in summer and chilly in winter).
  • Comfortable walking shoes to handle those 10,000 steps a day.
  • Cash in Japanese Yen for smaller shops and restaurants.
  • An umbrella or light rain jacket, especially during the rainy season.
  • Portable charger for your phone or camera.

Currency

Japan uses the Japanese Yen (¥). Many places in Osaka accept credit cards, but it’s wise to keep cash on hand for street vendors, smaller eateries, and local markets. ATMs in convenience stores often cater to international cards, making it relatively easy to withdraw Yen.

Best Time to Visit

Spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November) feature mild temperatures and beautiful scenery. Summer brings festivals and lively streets, while winter sees fewer crowds and a different, quieter charm. Plan according to your weather preference and any seasonal events you’d like to catch.

Fun Facts

  • Osaka’s nickname “Nation’s Kitchen” dates back to its role as Japan’s rice trade hub.
  • The city has a rich comedy culture, particularly manzai stand-up duos.
  • Shitennoji Temple, founded in 593 AD, is one of Japan’s oldest official temples.
  • Universal Studios Japan was the first Universal theme park to open outside the U.S.
  • Okonomiyaki and takoyaki are two of Osaka’s most beloved street foods.

Tips and Recommendations

Do’s and Don’ts

  • Do: Try local dishes like takoyaki and okonomiyaki.
  • Don’t: Litter or disrupt public spaces—cleanliness is valued here.
  • Do: Use Osaka’s efficient public transport, including subways and trains.
  • Don’t: Underestimate travel times; attractions can be large and spread out.
  • Do: Learn a few Japanese phrases—locals appreciate the effort.
  • Don’t: Rush. Take your time to immerse yourself in the culture.

Local Etiquette

Politeness and respect are deeply ingrained in Japanese culture. Here are a few quick tips:

  • Greet with a slight bow and a friendly “Konnichiwa.”
  • Remove shoes when entering homes or certain traditional establishments.
  • Offer or accept items with both hands as a sign of respect.
  • Speak quietly in public places to maintain a peaceful environment.
  • Dress modestly when visiting temples or shrines.
