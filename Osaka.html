Osaka

← Back to Destinations

What to See

Osaka, known as the "Nation's Kitchen," is renowned for its vibrant food scene, bustling nightlife, and historic landmarks. Visitors can explore the majestic Osaka Castle, wander through the lively Dotonbori district, and experience the unique blend of traditional and modern attractions that make Osaka a must-visit destination.

A Brief History of the City

Osaka has a rich history that dates back centuries, serving as a major economic hub and cultural center in Japan. The city played a pivotal role during the Edo period as a center for trade and industry. Despite suffering significant destruction during World War II, Osaka has rebuilt itself into a thriving metropolis known for its resilience, innovation, and dynamic spirit.

Top 10 Attractions

  1. Osaka Castle - A symbol of Osaka's history, offering panoramic views and beautiful surrounding parks.
  2. Dotonbori - A vibrant entertainment district famous for its neon lights, street food, and iconic Glico Man sign.
  3. Universal Studios Japan - A world-renowned theme park featuring attractions based on popular movies and franchises.
  4. Shinsaibashi Shopping Arcade - The largest shopping street in Osaka, perfect for retail therapy and dining.
  5. Umeda Sky Building - An architectural marvel with an open-air observation deck offering stunning city views.
  6. Tsutenkaku Tower - A historic tower in the Shinsekai district, offering observation decks and local attractions.
  7. Sumiyoshi Taisha - One of Japan's oldest Shinto shrines, known for its unique architecture.
  8. Osaka Aquarium Kaiyukan - One of the world's largest and most impressive aquariums, showcasing diverse marine life.
  9. Tempozan Ferris Wheel - A giant Ferris wheel providing breathtaking views of Osaka Bay and the city skyline.
  10. National Bunraku Theatre - Experience traditional Japanese puppet theatre at this dedicated venue.

What to Bring

  • Comfortable walking shoes for exploring Osaka's extensive attractions and bustling streets.
  • A lightweight jacket or sweater, as Osaka's weather can vary, especially in the evenings.
  • Sun protection: sunscreen, hats, and sunglasses to shield against Osaka's sunny days.
  • A reusable water bottle to stay hydrated during your adventures.
  • Camera or smartphone for capturing Osaka's vibrant scenes and memorable moments.
  • Guidebook or travel app to navigate the city's attractions and discover hidden gems.
  • Portable charger for your electronic devices to keep them powered throughout the day.
  • Umbrella or raincoat, as Osaka experiences frequent rain, especially during the rainy season.
  • Basic Japanese phrases guide to enhance communication with locals.
  • Cash in Japanese Yen, as some establishments may prefer cash over cards.

Currency

Osaka, like the rest of Japan, uses the Japanese Yen (¥). While credit and debit cards are widely accepted in urban areas, it's advisable to carry some cash for smaller establishments, street vendors, and rural areas where card payments may be limited.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Osaka is during the spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November) months when the weather is mild and the city is adorned with beautiful cherry blossoms and vibrant autumn foliage. Summer (June to August) is ideal for those who enjoy festivals and lively street activities, while winter (December to February) offers cooler temperatures and fewer tourists.

Fun Facts

  • Osaka is often referred to as the "Kitchen of Japan" due to its rich food culture and influence on Japanese cuisine.
  • The city is home to Japan's oldest surviving temple, Shitennoji, founded in 593 AD.
  • Osaka has a vibrant comedy scene, particularly known for its traditional manzai (stand-up) performances.
  • The Osaka Castle was originally built in the 16th century by the warlord Toyotomi Hideyoshi.
  • Osaka Aquarium Kaiyukan houses one of the world's largest collections of marine life, including whale sharks.
  • Universal Studios Japan in Osaka was the first Universal Studios theme park to open in Asia.
  • Osaka is twinned with several cities around the world, including San Francisco (USA), Melbourne (Australia), and Busan (South Korea).
  • The city boasts a high number of Michelin-starred restaurants, particularly renowned for its okonomiyaki and takoyaki.
  • Osaka's subway system is one of the most extensive and efficient in the world, facilitating easy exploration of the city.
  • Osaka is a major economic powerhouse in Japan, contributing significantly to the country's GDP.

Tips and Recommendations

Do’s and Don’ts

  • Do: Explore Osaka's diverse culinary scene and try local specialties like okonomiyaki and takoyaki.
  • Don’t: Litter or disturb public spaces. Osaka prides itself on its cleanliness.
  • Do: Utilize the efficient public transportation system, including subways and buses, to navigate the city.
  • Don’t: Underestimate the size of Osaka's attractions. Allocate sufficient time to fully enjoy each site.
  • Do: Learn basic Japanese phrases to enhance interactions with locals and enrich your travel experience.
  • Don’t: Rush through attractions. Take the time to immerse yourself in Osaka's culture and history.

Packing Lists

  • Comfortable walking shoes or sneakers for extensive exploration.
  • Lightweight, breathable clothing suitable for Osaka's humid climate.
  • Sun protection: sunscreen, hats, and sunglasses.
  • Reusable water bottle to stay hydrated throughout the day.
  • Camera or smartphone with ample storage for capturing memories.
  • Portable charger for electronic devices to keep them powered.
  • Rain jacket or umbrella for unexpected showers.
  • Basic first-aid kit for minor health needs.
  • Guidebook or travel app for navigation and information.
  • Cash in Japanese Yen for places that prefer cash payments.

Local Etiquette

Japanese culture emphasizes politeness, respect, and harmony. Here are some local etiquette tips to enhance your visit to Osaka:

  • Always greet with a polite bow and a friendly "Konnichiwa" (Hello).
  • Remove your shoes before entering someone's home or certain traditional establishments.
  • Handle money and items with both hands as a sign of respect.
  • Speak softly in public places to maintain a peaceful environment.
  • When visiting temples or shrines, follow the proper purification rituals and dress modestly.
