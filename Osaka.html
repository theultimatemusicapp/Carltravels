My Osaka Journey

About three months ago, I made the decision to overhaul my life: I left a job that wasn’t fulfilling, quit smoking, and cut out a few toxic influences along the way. Change can be tough—uncomfortable, even scary—but I’ve never felt more alive. Now I’m celebrating 100 days without a cigarette and feeling stronger than ever.

I’ve arrived in Osaka with a clear mission: work on my fitness, explore new horizons, and document every step of the journey. This isn’t a holiday—it’s a daily commitment to setting goals, pushing myself, and seeing what I’m really capable of. Whether I’m getting up before dawn to rack up 10,000 steps, tackling a new workout, or simply immersing myself in the city’s culture, each day is another opportunity to grow.

From the quiet magic of Tsutenkaku Tower at sunrise to the bustling neon streets at night, Osaka has already offered so many memorable moments. I ventured out to Nara Deer Park with new friends, fed the local deer, and stood in awe before the giant Buddha at Tōdai-ji Temple—an experience that made every sacrifice and change back home feel worthwhile. I even hopped on the Don Quijote Ferris wheel, taking in an unforgettable panoramic view of Osaka’s vibrant skyline.

In about five weeks, I’ll be heading to Okinawa to begin my karate training, and I’m determined to show up in the best shape possible—physically, mentally, and creatively. This is just the beginning, and I can’t wait to see where this path leads. Join me as I keep challenging myself, exploring new cities, and sharing my journey every step of the way.

What to See

Osaka, known as the “Nation’s Kitchen,” is renowned for its dynamic food culture, historic landmarks, and energetic nightlife. Stroll through Dotonbori’s neon-lit streets, visit the iconic Osaka Castle, and explore districts like Shinsekai or Shinsaibashi for a perfect blend of modern excitement and traditional charm.

A Brief History of the City

Osaka’s roots stretch back centuries. Once a vital hub during the Edo period, the city flourished as a center of trade, industry, and culinary innovation. Despite wartime destruction, Osaka rebuilt itself into a metropolis celebrated for its resilience and spirited approach to life—values still visible in its bustling streets and warm hospitality today.

Top 10 Attractions

  1. Osaka Castle – A historical fortress with sprawling grounds and panoramic city views.
  2. Dotonbori – Famous for its vibrant nightlife, street food, and iconic neon signs.
  3. Universal Studios Japan – A beloved theme park featuring world-class rides and shows.
  4. Shinsaibashi Shopping Arcade – A lively retail haven for fashion, food, and souvenirs.
  5. Umeda Sky Building – Marvel at the open-air observatory for breathtaking skyline vistas.
  6. Tsutenkaku Tower – An emblem of Osaka’s past and present, located in the nostalgic Shinsekai district.
  7. Sumiyoshi Taisha – One of Japan’s oldest Shinto shrines, rich in tradition.
  8. Osaka Aquarium Kaiyukan – Among the world’s largest aquariums, showcasing incredible marine life.
  9. Tempozan Ferris Wheel – Soar above Osaka Bay for panoramic water and city views.
  10. National Bunraku Theatre – Experience traditional Japanese puppet theatre in its finest form.

What to Bring

  • Comfortable shoes for exploring Osaka’s lively streets.
  • Season-appropriate clothing (winters can be cold, summers humid).
  • Umbrella or light rain jacket (showers can be sudden).
  • Portable charger for your phone or camera.
  • Cash in Japanese Yen for smaller shops and eateries.

Currency

Japan uses the Japanese Yen (¥). While many places in Osaka accept credit cards, carrying some cash is wise for street vendors, traditional markets, and smaller establishments.

Best Time to Visit

Spring (March–May) and autumn (September–November) are ideal for mild temperatures and seasonal beauty. If you enjoy festivals and lively street scenes, summer offers countless events. Winter is quieter, with cooler days but fewer crowds.

Fun Facts

  • Osaka’s nickname “Nation’s Kitchen” highlights its influence on Japanese cuisine.
  • The city boasts a thriving comedy culture, especially in the manzai style.
  • Shitennoji, founded in 593 AD, is considered one of Japan’s oldest temples.
  • Osaka Castle, originally built by Toyotomi Hideyoshi, remains a symbol of the city’s heritage.
  • Universal Studios Japan was the first Universal theme park to open in Asia.

Tips and Recommendations

Do’s and Don’ts

  • Do: Try local specialties like okonomiyaki and takoyaki.
  • Don’t: Litter or disturb public spaces—cleanliness is a point of pride.
  • Do: Use Osaka’s efficient subway system to get around easily.
  • Don’t: Rush. Allocate enough time to fully enjoy each attraction.
  • Do: Learn a few Japanese phrases—locals appreciate the effort.
  • Don’t: Skip cultural etiquette; respect local customs and traditions.

Local Etiquette

Japanese culture values politeness and respect. A few quick tips:

  • Offer a small bow and a friendly greeting like “Konnichiwa.”
  • Remove your shoes before entering traditional homes or temples.
  • Speak quietly in public to maintain a peaceful atmosphere.
  • Use both hands when giving or receiving items as a sign of respect.
  • Dress modestly when visiting religious sites.
